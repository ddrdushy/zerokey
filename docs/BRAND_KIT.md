# BRAND KIT — ZeroKey

> ZeroKey is a product brand sitting inside the Symprio family. It inherits Symprio's modern, confident enterprise aesthetic and adapts it for a younger, friendlier, more product-focused audience: Malaysian SME owners, finance managers, and bookkeepers.

## Brand architecture

ZeroKey is positioned as a **product of Symprio**, not a separate company. The relationship is the same shape as Atlassian → Jira, Microsoft → Copilot, or Stripe → Atlas. Symprio is the parent house brand carrying enterprise credibility, four-country presence, and partnerships with UiPath, Microsoft, Oracle, Google, Meta, and Anthropic. ZeroKey is the productized, self-serve, SME-accessible tip of that spear.

In practice this means three distinct brand expressions exist simultaneously and each is used in the right context.

The **ZeroKey wordmark alone** is used on the product itself, in self-serve marketing surfaces, in SME-facing content, and anywhere the audience does not need to be told who built it. This is the dominant expression and accounts for roughly eighty percent of all touchpoints.

The **"ZeroKey by Symprio" lockup** is used on enterprise-facing surfaces: BFSI sales decks, RFP responses, procurement documentation, ISO certification materials, formal contracts, partnership announcements, and the footer of every marketing page. This is where the Symprio credibility transfer happens.

The **Symprio mark referencing ZeroKey as a product** appears on Symprio's own website (symprio.com/products/zerokey or equivalent), in Symprio sales motions where ZeroKey is positioned as one of several Symprio offerings, and in PR around the Symprio business. This is rare but consequential.

The boundary is enforced by context, not by rule. When in doubt, default to the ZeroKey wordmark alone and add Symprio attribution only when the audience actively benefits from knowing.

## Brand personality

ZeroKey's personality is a deliberate calibration: confident enough to be trusted with tax-sensitive work, warm enough to be inviting to a non-technical SME owner, modern enough to feel like a product from 2026 rather than a procurement system from 2012, and quietly competent enough to never need to shout about itself.

If ZeroKey were a person, they would be a senior product manager at a respected tech company who happens to have grown up in Malaysia, speaks all four languages comfortably, has worked through three regulatory mandates at large institutions, and now wants to build the tool they wished existed for their parents' SME. They are calm under pressure. They explain technical things in plain language without being condescending. They have nothing to prove. They notice when you are stressed and adjust their tone accordingly.

The traits we cultivate are **calm, confident, useful, honest, warm**. The traits we explicitly avoid are **loud, breathless, jargon-heavy, condescending, salesy, panicky, fear-based, manipulative**.

The Symprio parent brand is more enterprise-formal in tone — appropriate for selling consulting hours to corporate buyers. ZeroKey is meaningfully warmer and more approachable than its parent. This is intentional. The audience is different. A bookkeeper in Klang reading our website needs to feel welcomed, not impressed.

## The verbal identity

The single sentence we lead with everywhere: **Drop the PDF. Drop the Keys.**

This is more than a tagline. It is a verbal artifact that does three jobs simultaneously. It tells the user what they need to do (drop a file). It tells them what we will do for them (everything else, including the cryptographic signing). And it carries the security message at a second layer of meaning that becomes important the moment a careful buyer pauses to read it again.

Below the tagline, three supporting promises form the secondary verbal hierarchy: **e-invoicing on autopilot**, **ten-minute setup**, **no consultants required**. These exist as pull-quotes, hero subheadings, and proof points throughout marketing surfaces.

The product name itself — ZeroKey — is always written as one word, with capital Z and capital K, never spaced, never hyphenated, never lowercased except inside URLs (zerokey.symprio.com) or code identifiers. In Bahasa Malaysia, Mandarin, and Tamil contexts the name remains in Latin characters as ZeroKey, never transliterated.

## Voice and tone

Voice is what stays constant across every surface. Tone is what shifts based on context.

The voice is **clear, calm, and quietly confident**. We use short sentences when a short sentence does the job. We use longer sentences when we need to explain something properly. We never sacrifice meaning for brevity, and we never pad meaning with unnecessary words.

We use the second person (you, your) almost always when speaking to the customer. We use the first person plural (we, our) when speaking about ZeroKey. We avoid the corporate third person ("ZeroKey enables enterprises to..."). We are not afraid to be direct: "Your invoice is ready. We sent it to LHDN. Here is the QR code."

We never use jargon as a flex. When a regulatory term is necessary, we use it once and explain it once, then keep using it. "Your TIN — that is your Tax Identification Number from LHDN — is missing." The next time TIN appears in the same flow, it appears alone.

We do not use empty intensifiers. Things are not "incredibly fast", "absolutely seamless", or "truly revolutionary". They are fast, or they are not. They work, or they do not. The customer can tell the difference.

We do not use fear or panic as a marketing lever, even though our market is full of forced buyers under regulatory pressure. We are honest about deadlines and consequences when asked. We do not put a doomsday clock in the navigation bar. The customer is anxious enough; our job is to be the calm in the room, not amplify the noise.

Tone shifts based on where the user is in their journey and what state they are in.

On the marketing site, the tone is **inviting and confident**. The first impression is "this looks like the right place" not "this looks like another tax software vendor".

On the empty-state of the product after signup, the tone is **welcoming and instructive**. The user just made a decision; we want them to feel rewarded for it. "Welcome. Drop your first invoice anywhere — drag it here, email it to your inbox, or send it on WhatsApp. We will take it from there."

In success states (invoice validated, TIN verified, batch completed), the tone is **quiet and matter-of-fact**, not celebratory. "Sent to LHDN. ID generated. Here is the QR code." We do not over-celebrate routine work; that feels patronizing.

In error states, the tone is **calm, plain, and helpful**. We explain what is wrong in language the user can understand, not in LHDN error codes. We always show the user what to do next. We never blame the user, even when the user is wrong. "We could not read the buyer's TIN from this PDF. Type it in here, or paste it from your records."

In educational and content surfaces (blog posts, guides, decoder articles), the tone is **authoritative without being lecturing**. We are confident enough in our subject matter to be brief. We link out to LHDN's official sources rather than paraphrasing them.

In communications during outages or incidents, the tone is **direct and accountable**. We name what happened, what we did, what we are still doing, and when we expect resolution. We do not hide behind passive voice or vague wording.

## Language support

ZeroKey ships in four languages from day one: **English, Bahasa Malaysia, Mandarin (Simplified), and Tamil**. English is the primary language for source content; the other three are first-class translations, not afterthoughts. The product surface, support documentation, error messages, marketing site, and contractual emails are all available in all four.

The translation philosophy is **adaptive, not literal**. A direct word-for-word translation of "Drop the PDF" into Tamil is awkward; the right Tamil headline expresses the same idea in a way a Tamil speaker would naturally say it. We hire human translators who are native speakers of the target language and Malaysian residents, and we treat their judgment as authoritative within their language.

The product name "ZeroKey" stays in Latin script across all languages. Brand-level slogans like "Drop the PDF. Drop the Keys." are localized properly, not transliterated. Numbers, currency, and dates follow Malaysian conventions consistently across languages: RM for currency, DD/MM/YYYY for dates, comma as thousands separator.

## Words we use

Several technical terms appear so often that consistency matters. The list below is the canonical form we use everywhere.

For the LHDN system, we always say **MyInvois** (one word, capital M and capital I), never "MyInvoice" or "My Invoice".

For the customer's signing certificate, we say **digital certificate** in user-facing text. We say **signing key** only in security or developer-facing surfaces. We never say "private key" to non-technical users.

For an e-invoice, we say **e-invoice** with hyphen, never "einvoice" or "e Invoice".

For the unique identifier LHDN returns on a validated invoice, we say **invoice ID** in user-facing surfaces and **UUID** only in developer documentation.

For the customer's collection of buyers and suppliers, we say **customer master** consistently. For their collection of products and services, we say **item master**. These terms are familiar to the SME audience.

For the people who use ZeroKey on behalf of their clients, we say **bookkeepers and accountants** — not "tax professionals" or "advisors". We respect the actual job titles of our audience.

For LHDN itself, we say **LHDN** (the Malay acronym) in primary text rather than the English "Inland Revenue Board". When clarification is needed, we say "LHDN — the Inland Revenue Board of Malaysia" once per document.

## Words we avoid

Some words appear so often in this category that they have become noise. We deliberately avoid them.

We do not say **revolutionary, game-changing, disruptive, cutting-edge, world-class, best-in-class, robust, seamless, leverage, synergy, solution-oriented, holistic, end-to-end** as marketing words. They are empty. If our product genuinely is revolutionary, the customer will say so; we do not need to.

We do not say **AI-powered** as a branding badge on every surface. AI is the implementation, not the value. We say what the AI does — extracts the buyer's TIN automatically, suggests the right MSIC code, learns from your corrections — and let the customer infer that this requires AI.

We do not say **enterprise-grade** in customer-facing marketing aimed at SMEs. We show it through ISO certifications, audit log capabilities, and the Symprio relationship. The phrase itself signals "this is too expensive for me" to an SME ear.

We do not use **military terminology** (mission-critical, weapon, deploy at scale, conquer the market). Tax compliance is not warfare.

We do not use **scarcity manipulation** (only 24 hours left, last chance, prices going up tomorrow). The LHDN deadline creates real urgency on its own; we do not need to manufacture more.

## Voice examples in real product surfaces

A few canonical examples of how the voice manifests in actual product copy.

When the customer first lands on the dashboard after signup, the empty state says: "Welcome, [Name]. Drop your first invoice to get started. We accept PDFs, photos, Excel, screenshots, or anything from your supplier. Drag a file here, or use one of these channels."

When an extracted invoice has a low-confidence field, the review screen says: "We were not sure about this field. Have a look and confirm or change it." (Not: "Error: confidence below threshold. Manual review required.")

When LHDN rejects an invoice for a missing TIN, the user sees: "LHDN could not validate this invoice. The buyer's TIN seems to be incorrect. Try the verified TIN we found, or paste the right one here." (Not: "Validation error 4001: TIN mismatch.")

When a user upgrades from Starter to Growth, the confirmation says: "You're on Growth now. Five hundred invoices per month included. Welcome." (Not: "Subscription upgrade successful. Your billing has been adjusted.")

When something genuinely goes wrong on our end, the status page or in-product banner says: "We are having trouble reaching MyInvois right now. Your invoices are saved and we will submit them as soon as the connection is back. We will email you when it is resolved." (Not: "Service degradation detected. Investigating.")

## Email and external communications

Transactional emails are short, useful, and signed simply: "— ZeroKey, made by Symprio in Kuala Lumpur." We sign in this way deliberately to humanize the product and reinforce the parent relationship without making it the headline.

Customer support replies are written in the same voice as the product. We do not switch to corporate-speak for ticket responses. We always say what we did, what we found, and what happens next.

Outage and incident communications are direct. The first line states what is broken. The second line states the impact on the customer. The third line states what we are doing. We add detail in subsequent paragraphs. We never lead with apology language; we lead with information.

## How ZeroKey behaves when things go wrong

Our handling of failure is the highest-leverage moment for brand reputation in our category. Customers under audit, customers facing penalties, customers whose business depends on a working invoice — these moments are when brand trust is built or destroyed.

When LHDN's MyInvois platform itself is down, we say so plainly. We do not blame the customer's input. We tell them their invoices are queued and will go through automatically when MyInvois is back. We email them when it is resolved. We do not require them to retry manually.

When our own system has a bug or outage, we acknowledge it on our status page and in-product immediately. We do not hide behind "scheduled maintenance" framing for unscheduled events. We do post-incident reviews publicly when the impact warranted it.

When a customer is on the verge of churn, we do not deploy retention pressure. We ask them what is wrong, fix it if we can, and let them leave gracefully if we cannot. The thirty-day money-back guarantee is honored without question. Our reputation in this market depends on never being the company that made cancellation hard.
